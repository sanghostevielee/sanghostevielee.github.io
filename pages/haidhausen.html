Au-Haidhausen

“

DASHA A piece of France outside France.
STEVIE There's a merry-go-round in Wiener Platz!
LIAM Cool because I live here.

”

The 5th borough of Germany, a land of young couples, yuppies, and beauty – one might assume it is just another gentrified neighbourhood surrounding Munich, but it is so much more. While many neighbourhoods emphasize their nightlife or shops, which are irrelevant in times of lockdown, we were happy to spend our day entirely outdoors in Au-Haidhausen, barely spending a penny.

Despite only a fifth of its buildings existing prior to World War I, it somehow manages to exude historical charm like few other areas of Munich and is a true joy to walk around – we started with the Maximilianeum, home of the Bavarian State parliament, it is surrounded by lovely nature on the Isar river, but it is of course stunning all on its own, built in 1949 but heavily influenced by Neo-Gothic palaces of the 18th Century.

Our glance at statehood was swiftly followed by exploring old winding streets on our way to Wiener Platz, a beautiful old town square that hosts an iconic Maypole – while many places here are currently closed, we could still grab a bite to eat and drink and enjoy the squares charm.

Nearby is the Kreichbaumhof, a famous old hostel that looks like a gorgeous old cabin, overgrown with greenery, it now serves as a clubhouse for Alpine youth members, but it was formerly an old hostel built around the 1800’s, and I chose it as the icon to represent this neighbourhood as it’s the largest of many 19th century gems in the area, in fact there is an entire small village made up of similarly small & old buildings, which now have an array of functions from kindergartens to museums!

As we headed further South, we discovered central Haidhausen, famously regarded as the French quarter of Munich, named for its layout, general sexiness, and apparently to remind French frogs who’s boss, as many of the streets here were specifically named after locations of victorious German battles in the Franco-Prussian war of the 19th Century.

I’ve mentioned just a few of the main beauties around Au-Haidhausen, but there are many more from Mariahilf Church to Loreto Chapel, that you will have to discover on your own.
